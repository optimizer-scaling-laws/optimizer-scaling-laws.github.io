Same Architecture, Different Capacity:
Optimizer-Induced Spectral Scaling Laws

Nandan Kumar Jha, Brandon Reagen
New York University
arXiv Code (coming soon)
TL;DR

Realized representation capacity is not architecture-only; it emerges from the architecture–optimizer pair. Optimizer geometry changes how added FFN width becomes usable capacity, and in most regimes its effect exceeds controlled architectural interventions.

Figure 1: Spectral scaling exponents depend on optimizer choice: Soft (left) and hard spectral rank (right) as a function of FFN width in GPT-2 160M. AdamW exhibits the largest hard–soft asymmetry (Δ1,2 = 0.37), indicating concentrated eigenspectra. Muon and Dion (1/2) reduce this asymmetry to Δ1,2 ≈ 0.14. Hard-rank scaling exhibits stronger dependence on optimizer choices.

Abstract

Scaling laws have made language-model performance predictable from model size, data, and compute, but they typically treat the optimizer as a fixed training detail. We show that this assumption misses a fundamental axis of representation scaling: how effectively the optimizer converts added FFN width into utilized spectral capacity. Using eigenspectra of feed-forward network representations, measured through soft and hard spectral-ranks, we find that the same Transformer architecture realizes markedly different spectral scaling laws when trained with different optimizers. Holding architecture and width schedule fixed, AdamW exhibits weak hard-rank scaling (β=0.44) on rare-token (TAIL) representations where learning is known to be hardest, whereas Muon achieves linear scaling (β=1.02) in the same regimes, a 2.3× increase in the scaling exponent. This difference is not reducible to validation loss: AdamW configurations can match low-rank Dion variants in perplexity, under extended training, while exhibiting sharply different spectral geometry, demonstrating that matched loss does not imply matched representation structure. Hard–soft rank asymmetry further reveals that optimizers differ not only in how much capacity is realized, but also in how that capacity is structured across eigenmodes. To disentangle optimizer effects from architectural ones, we compare against architectural interventions (e.g., attention rank and positional encoding), and find that optimizer-induced spectral shifts often exceed the architectural effects. These results suggest optimization as a first-class axis of representation scaling, motivating optimizer–architecture co-design.

Key Findings

1Optimizer-Induced Spectral Scaling Laws

The same Transformer architecture realizes substantially different FFN spectral-capacity scaling laws depending on the optimizer. Hard-rank scaling spans β ∈ [0.29, 0.82] across optimizers, while soft-rank scaling is clustered in β ∈ [0.66, 1.01]. The hard–soft asymmetry is itself optimizer-dependent — a property of the architecture–optimizer pair, not architecture alone.

2Matched Loss ≠ Matched Geometry

AdamW trained for 12K steps matches Dion(1/16) in perplexity, but its aggregate hard-rank scaling collapses to β=0.03. Neither learning-rate tuning nor extended training closes the spectral gap. Configurations with matched loss can exhibit distinct spectral geometries.

3Frequency-Dependent Capacity Allocation

Optimizer-induced scaling varies across token-frequency regimes. The largest AdamW-to-Muon gain appears in mid-frequency tokens (Δβ = +0.69), not tail tokens. The optimizer changes not only total capacity but how it is allocated across the token distribution.

4Update Rank as a Capacity Control Knob

Dion rank sweeps show that reducing update rank from r=1/2 to r=1/16 drops TAIL hard-rank scaling from β=0.88 to β=0.40. Orthonormalization alone is insufficient — the rank of the optimizer update constrains dominant-mode hard capacity.

5Optimizer Effects Exceed Architectural Interventions

Optimizer-induced spectral shifts exceed attention-rank architectural shifts in 28 of 30 comparisons. Removing RoPE produces optimizer-dependent redistribution — the same architectural change has opposite effects under different optimizers.

6Optimizer–Architecture Co-Design

Architectural interventions do not induce fixed, optimizer-independent spectral shifts. Increasing per-head attention rank changes which optimizer achieves the best scaling exponent. Optimizer geometry also expands the trainable architecture space — Muon-family trains PostLN-75 (PPL=32.8) where AdamW diverges.

Spectral Scaling Across Token-Frequency Regimes

We perform spectral scaling analysis separately across token-frequency regimes to see how capacity is allocated for frequent vs. rare tokens. Hard-rank scaling is strongly optimizer-dependent, with the MID and TAIL tokens being the most diagnostic regimes for optimizer-induced scaling effects.

Matched Loss Does Not Imply Matched Geometry

Extended AdamW training matches Dion(1/16) in validation perplexity, but hard-rank scaling collapses — βhard drops from 0.29 to 0.03. Soft-rank scaling remains relatively stable, but hard–soft asymmetry grows from +0.37 to +0.55. This shows that optimizer-induced spectral scaling is not a transient convergence artifact.

Figure 3: Extended AdamW training weakens hard-rank scaling. Hard-rank scaling (βhard) exhibits diminishing return for TAIL token scaling, while soft-rank scaling remains comparatively stable, leading to increasing hard–soft asymmetry.

Update Rank Constrains Hard-Capacity Scaling

Dion provides a controlled intervention: varying the rank fraction r changes the rank of the projected update while preserving orthonormalized-update structure. As rank decreases, TAIL hard-rank scaling drops sharply while soft-rank degrades more gradually.

Figure 4: TAIL-token spectral scaling for Dion rank sweep. Hard-rank scaling drops from β=0.88 at r=1/2 to β=0.40 at r=1/16, falling into the AdamW regime. Soft-rank scaling degrades more gradually (0.95 → 0.72) and remains above AdamW.

Scale Persistence at 350M

The optimizer-dependent structure persists at 350M scale. Muon achieves near-linear hard-rank scaling (βhard = 1.13, R2 = 0.94), while AdamW remains clearly sublinear (βhard = 0.39, R2 = 0.82).

Figure 5: Optimizer-dependent TAIL spectral scaling persists at 350M scale. Muon and NorMuon maintain stronger hard-rank scaling than AdamW, while low-rank Dion(1/16) remains in a lower hard-capacity regime.

Optimizer Effects Dominate Architectural Interventions

We compare optimizer-induced spectral scaling shifts against the effect of increasing per-head attention rank at fixed parameter count. Optimizer-induced gains exceed attention-rank shifts in 28 of 30 comparisons.

Figure 6: Optimizer-induced shifts in spectral-scaling exceed attention-rank shifts in GPT-2 160M. Optimizer-induced gains (Δβ★opt, red dashed lines) exceed attention-rank shifts (bars) in 28 of 30 comparisons (marked with ★).

Rényi-Family Analysis: Where Optimizer-Induced Capacity Forms

The Rényi effective-rank family reveals that AdamW’s FFN nonlinearity must perform 10–12× larger compensatory reinjection than Muon’s, yet still achieves the lowest post-activation rank. Large reinjection is compensatory, not beneficial — it indicates the optimizer has created a more collapsed pre-activation geometry.

Figure 7: Rényi-family view of optimizer-shaped spectral capacity in GPT-2 350M. Pre-activation rank (left), post-activation rank (middle), and nonlinear reinjection ratio (right). The strongest optimizer separation appears before the FFN nonlinearity.

Hard-Rank Dynamics Under Extended Training

Extended AdamW training disrupts the width–capacity ordering itself. The 8d model peaks early, then drops below narrower models — breaking the monotonic relationship needed for a power-law fit, despite continuously improving validation perplexity.

Related Work

This paper builds on our prior work on spectral analysis of LLM representations:

NerVE: Nonlinear Eigenspectrum Dynamics in LLM Feed-Forward Networks

Published at ICLR 2026. NerVE showed that FFN nonlinearities reinject variance across eigenmodes, and that optimizer geometry modulates the extent of this reinjection. The present work extends this analysis to the scaling regime.

Spectral Scaling Laws in Language Models: How Effectively Do Feed-Forward Networks Use Their Latent Space?

Published at EMNLP 2025. Established spectral scaling laws for FFN latent-space utilization under a fixed optimizer. The present work shows that these scaling laws are not invariant to optimizer choice.
